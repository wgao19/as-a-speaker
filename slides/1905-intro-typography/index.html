Gutenberg

First, let me introduce to you "Gutenberg". The term _Gutenberg_ nowadays represents a lot of things.

Aaaaand of a person who lived ~600 years ago. So who was Gutenberg. Why is he naming all these things? I guess we'll need to learn about his contributions in his time.

Gutenberg lived in the 15th century Germany, city of Mainz, near today's Frankfurt. His dad was a patrician merchant, patrician back then meaning he had certain societary recognition, was probably wealthy and political elite. It was believed that his dad was a goldsmith, one who makes gold articles, before he started trading.

Most of the early life of Gutenberg himself was unknown, except the fact that he was probably familiar with the trade of goldsmithing.

In the early 1400, Gutenberg's family moved to Strassberg likely due to political reasons. He was found in records to have gone to school, possibly married, enrolled as a goldsmith in the Strasbourg militia, as well as inheriting proceedings when his father passed away.
It was also speculated that it was within the time he stayed in Strasbourg that he was formulating idea of movable type printing.
I should pause here and mention that Bi Sheng of the Song dynasty of China was probably the earliest inventor of movable type printing. That dated back to around 1000. But in Europe it was still at the stage of an idea in the 15th century.
It was believed that Gutenberg had perfected his research and experimentation on movable type printing around year of 1440. And by the time he moved back to Mainz in 1448, he took a loan from his brother-in-law, likely to start a printing shop.

One major innovation of Gutenberg was apparently adapting the idea of his movable type printing method.

The first part involved using a single, hand-carved character to create identical copies of itself. Each of those hand-carved character that will be used to create copies are called a "master piece" of a type.

Cutting one single letter would take a craftsman a day of work, it was obviously impractical to be carving all the copies by hand. Copies were produced by stamping the original into an iron plate, called a matrix.

Here is a matrix, a matrix for a font we'll be talking about later.

A rectangular tube was then connected to the matrix, creating a container in which molten type metal could be poured. Once cooled, the solid metal form was released from the tube. The fundamental innovation is that this matrix can be used to produce many duplicates of the same letter.

It follows that when printing, you'd be arranging your types into words
including ligatures, and

by the way, if you notice the metal strip beneath the actual letters, that transformed into what we current days refer to as "leading", a measurement in typography that describes the distance between two baselines of text.

So you'd be arranging your letters into words, then blocks of texts, and eventually apply ink to the surface and press a paper against it, and make it to a final print.

what's more, Gutenberg was known to have done text justification already, by using characters of narrower widths, adding extra spaces around punctuation, and varying the widths of spaces around words.

This is one page of one of the masterpieces that Gutenberg printed, the 42-line bible. 42-line meaning the page is laid out to print maximum of 42 lines on each page.

Some would describe it as one of the most beautiful books ever printed.

Gutenberg was later on involved in a mortgage with a merchant, Johann Fust. At some point the cost of producing the 42-page bible proliferated, the mortgage went up, and Fust requested his money back, sued, and foreclosed. Gutenberg had to forfeit all his equipment.

Fust and his son-in-law later finished the project, and continued printing and publishing with great success.

Fust died in 1466. Gutenberg died penniless two years later.

His truly epochal invention was the combination of these (different printing methods) elements into a practical system that allowed the mass production of printed books and was economically viable for printers and readers alike.

Development

After proven practical by Gutenberg's print shops, printing started to evolve and spread in the land of Europe.

Here in this diagram, it marks the progression in colored dots by decades.

In Gutenberg's decade, only Mainz and Strassburg had a meaningful number of print shop.

A decade later, marked by the blue dots, major cities such as Paris and Venice joined the club, as well as serving as the "diffusion centers" for even more printing shops for the future.

By 1481, barely 30 years after the publication of the 42-line Bible, even smallen nation such as Netherlands already featured printing shops in 21 cities and towns

And there're probably a few things we can conclude from this phenomena, it must have been very lucrative. But I guess a more intereting one was probably that there evolved different styles and opinions of the art of printing. Likewise how we nowadays evolved many styles of coding.

Here is one produced by a fifteen century Italian "punchcutter" named Francesco Griffo, one who literally punches in steel to make the letter types, was aiming to creating a more aesthetically even texture on the page.

Francesco worked for a Venetian printer Aldus Manutius, who founded the Aldine Press, of which the first great achievement was a five-volumn folio edition of Aristotle.
Later on, Aldus hired Francesco Griffo to create a typeface designed to reproduce the written hand of the humanists.

Giovanni Antonio Tagliente

Francesco was then inspired by a writing manual by Giovanni Antonio Tagliente, and created his cutting.

This resulted in the first roman face adapted and known today as italic type.

The first printing done with his cutting was a book called De Aetna, a 60-page text that depicts a journey to Mount Etna, written by an Italian poet Pietro Bembo.

This is how the font we use today as Bembo got its name.

Bembo characterises an elegant tail on the Q that starts from the glyph's center, the uppercase J has a hook and the the uppercase A has a flat top.

Here is a modern recreation of Bembo by a company named Monotype.

And here is part of its specimen. In a specimen, you specify all the glyphs, in different sizes, which normally are measured in points, and 72 points make an inch.

We got, upper case, upper case italic, lower case roman, lower case italic, upper and lower case numerals, punctuations, letters with decorators, and ligatures

If you notice there is a group where the letter forms look like capital letters, but smaller. Those are small caps, they are of same height of lowercase, and they are most often used to stress text without breaking flow of its surroundings.

Let me sneak in some more typographical terms here. Typographers refer to the height of lowercase letters by "x-height", understandably, that'll be the height of the letter lowercase x.

So small caps are capital letters that match x-height.

Then the imaginary line that sits on top of x will be the "median".

And those strokes that extend beyond x-height are ascenders and descenders.

And furthermore, the terms "upper" and "lower" case do not actually mean bigger or smaller letters.

With this another specimen of Bembo where we can observe more closely, the uppercase are letter forms of same height.

And lowercase are ones with ascenders and descenders.

They were originally called "majuscule" v.s. "miniscule", and the distinction applies to the numerals as well.

So where did the term uppercase and lowercase come from? Turns out,

Turns out, they refer to the cases that were used to hold those metal types.

They were called the "job cases". Originally one large case was used for each typeface, then "divided cases", pairs of cases for majuscules and minuscules, were introduced sixteenth centry. And the term upper and lower case came from this division.

Coming back to our original specimen, each of those type had a master piece of glyph cut by hands, let's appreciate that that is truly massive amount of work by typographers. Artistic masterpieces by craftsmen.

The types he created are more expressive, looked more like a caligrapher's pen, compared with Venetian style that looked more stiff, as you may see here most spectacularly via the r.

Garamond features a small eye in the lowercase `e` that is perhaps the most distinctive feature of the font.

Garamond represents a genre in typography common referred to as the "old style"

Old style features a diagonal stress, relatively low or moderate contrast between thin and thick strokes, wedged serifs, and those on lowercase letters are slanted,

Baskerville marks the genre of transitional, where it emphasized a perpendicular stress, high contrast between thick and think strokes, and a less slanted serifs

Baskerville's innovations exerted a notable influence on European typefounders. Let's take a look at this font called Bodoni.

Bodoni was an Italian typographer in late eighteenth century Italy. He was born in a printer family, both his father and his grandfather were printers. And as a child his toys were his grandfather's leftover punches and matrices. He grew up having an ambition of becoming a printer.

Bodoni traveled to Rome where he started as an assistant compositor, a.k.a. typesetter, one who sets the types to make prints. From my understanding this role requires a taste in aesthetics similar to one who does typography design of a webpage now. And Bodoni was apparently talented, he soon became the press's compositor of foreign languages, and started to typeset books.

His fellows were so delighted with his work on the "Pontificale Arabo-Copto" that they allowed him to add his name and birthplace to subsequent printings. He then began cutting his own punches.

Didot, on the other hand, was born in Paris, 24 years after Bodoni. But he was also born in a printers' family.

He invented both the process and the word "stereotype", where you mold a metal plate of set so you don't have to set the page again for reuse.

That is a separate story, but Firmin Didot also designed his own types, which is the choice of font in this slides. His design and Bodoni's together are known as "Didone", which is an amalgamation of their surnames.

Didone types' goals were to create more elegant designs of printed text.

It carried forward the technological advances began by Baskerville, showing off his delicate type to best advantage.

Although apparently influenced by Baskervill, they are a new design featuring unbracketed serifs, and an even more extreme contrast of thick and thin strokes.

Their style also significantly influenced display types.

And continues to be influential today.

The industrial revolution of the 19th century changed printing, typesetting, and type-casting from the product of the human hand to the product of power-driven machinery. Thousands of copies could be printed in the time it formerly took to print dozens.

It also created a market of consumers for manufacturers. The requirement to advertise products to mass consumers drove printers began to distinguish between book printing and commercial printing.

In manuscripts, typographers had often produced oversized letterforms to provide color and contrast on the page.
Another technique was to have squared serifs -- serifs to have the same weight as the strokes of the letterform itself.
Then we have yet another -- to get rid of the serifs at all. Here we have Futura which is distinctly geometric.
