Aaaaand of a person who lived ~600 years ago. So who was Gutenberg. Why is he naming all these things? I guess we'll need to learn about his contributions in his time.

Gutenberg lived in the 15th century Germany. He was a blacksmith and goldsmith. Printing at that time was a new idea. And "printer" was a profession.

By 1436 he started experimenting with a new technology which later he redesigned to become the European version of movable type.

Just as a side note, movable type was first invented by Bi Sheng as one of the four greatest ancient inventions of China.

Gutenberg lived in the 15th century Germany, city of Mainz, near today's Frankfurt. His dad was a patrician merchant, patrician back then meaning he had certain societary recognition, was probably wealthy and political elite. It was believed that his dad was a goldsmith, one who makes gold articles, before he started trading.

Most of the early life of Gutenberg himself was unknown, except the fact that he was probably familiar with the trade of goldsmithing.

In the early 1400, Gutenberg's family moved to Strassberg likely due to political reasons. He was found in records to have gone to school, possibly married, enrolled as a goldsmith in the Strasbourg militia, as well as inheriting proceedings when his father passed away.
One major innovation of Gutenberg was apparently adapting the idea of his movable type printing method. The first part involved using a single, hand-carved character to create identical copies of itself. Cutting one single letter would take a craftsman a day of work, it was obviously impractical to be carving all the copies by hand. Copies were produced by stamping the original into an iron plate, called a matrix, A rectangular tube was then connected to the matrix, creating a container in which molten type metal could be poured. Once cooled, the solid metal form was released from the tube. The fundamental innovation is that this matrix can be used to produce many duplicates of the same letter.

It was also speculated that it was within the time he stayed in Strasbourg that he was formulating idea of movable type printing.
I should pause here and mention that Bi Sheng of the Song dynasty of China was probably the earliest inventor of movable type printing. That dated back to around 1000. But in Europe it was still at the stage of an idea in the 15th century.
It was believed that he had perfected his research and experimentation on movable type printing around year of 1440. And by the time he moved back to Mainz in 1448, he took a loan from his brother-in-law, likely to start a printing shop.

His business was later involved in a mortgage with a merchant Johann Fust. At some point the mortgage went up to 2,000 guilders, when Fust foreclosed. And Gutenberg had to forfeit all his equipment.

The merchant and his son-in-law later finished the project, and continued printing and publishing with great success.

Fust died in 1466. Gutenberg died penniless two years later.

His truly epochal invention was the combination of these (different printing methods) elements into a practical system that allowed the mass production of printed books and was economically viable for printers and readers alike.

California job case

Modern, factory-produced movable type was available in the late 19th century. It was held in the printing shop in a job case, a drawer about 2 inches high, a yard wide, and about two feet deep, with many small compartments for the various letters and ligatures.
A California job case is a kind of type case. It was the most popular and accepted of the job case designs in America.

Describing letter forms

Here are some of the terms to describe type faces.

The x-height is the height of the lower case x.

The median is the imaginary line that sits on top of that x.

Curved strokes would normally go slightly higher than the median because human eyes perceive circles as smaller than squares.

The parts that extend beyond x-height are ascenders and descenders.

Then cap height is the height of capital letters.

The ratio between x-height and cap height may vary between different fonts. And the difference would give you a very different visual effect.

The font of larger x-height may look bigger even though they may be of same font size.

A type is traditionally measured in points. 1 inch has 72 points, where 12 pts is 1 pica. 6 picas 7 points often written as 6p7.
Set width talks about how wide each type is. Mono width that a lot of us are pretty excited about, is a category of fonts where each type is of same set width.

Caslon's type was distributed throughout England's American colonies, and was the typeface used in the first printed copies of the US Declaration of Independence and Constitution.

The computer version of Caslon, Adobe Caslon, introduced in 1990, was designed by Carol Twombly from the original Caslon specimen sheets of 1738 and 1786.

Baskerville

The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog

Now comes to a personal favorite.

At the turn of the 18th century, Philippe Grandjean (French) was almost a decade into his production of a font for Louis 14's royal press. This project carried on past his deth.

The characters featured thin, unbracketed serifs, extreme contrast between thick and think strokes, a perpendicular stress (as you can see the comparison here against bembo), the typeface marked a clear departure from the types of Griffo and Garamond.

As a personal property of the king, the font by Philippe Grandjean could not be used by commercial printers. But enterprising typefounders began copying almost immediately. And the most successful application came from John Baskerville.

Display Fonts

The industrial revolution of the 19th century changed printing, typesetting, and type-casting from the product of the human hand to the product of power-driven machinery. Thousands of copies could be printed in the time it formerly took to print dozens.

It also created a market of consumers for manufacturers. The requirement to advertise products to mass consumers drove printers began to distinguish between book printing and commercial printing.

In manuscripts, typographers had often produced oversized letterforms to provide color and contrast on the page.
Another technique was to have squared serifs -- serifs to have the same weight as the strokes of the letterform itself.
Then we have yet another -- to get rid of the serifs at all. Here we have Futura which is distinctly geometric.
